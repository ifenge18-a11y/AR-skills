# Writing Workflow

## Use For

Use this reference for accounting, finance, and financial management manuscript text: abstracts, introductions, theory sections, hypotheses, research-design prose, result narratives, contribution framing, and revision of existing draft sections.

This reference does not cover publication strategy, journal targeting, cover letters, reviewer responses, rebuttals, seminar slides, PPT decks, Nature/CNS-style generic prose, or biomedical writing.

## Intake Check

Before drafting or revising, identify:

- Section type: abstract, introduction, theory, hypotheses, research design, results narrative, contribution paragraph, or revision of existing prose.
- Research question, setting, unit of analysis, and inference goal.
- Core evidence: literature support, empirical design, supplied results, or author notes.
- Claim boundary: association, causal estimate, mechanism evidence, measurement contribution, or descriptive mapping.
- Language target: Chinese planning prose, English manuscript prose, or bilingual support.

If results, coefficients, sample size, tests, or cited findings are missing, use placeholders or state the missing inputs. Do not invent them.

## Section Patterns

### Abstract

Use this order:

`phenomenon -> research gap -> setting/design -> main evidence -> contribution -> boundary`

Keep claims proportional to the design. Include quantitative results only when the user provides them.

### Introduction

Use this order:

`phenomenon -> accounting relevance -> unresolved tension -> research question -> design/data -> findings if supplied -> contribution`

A strong introduction should explain why the question matters specifically for accounting, finance, or financial management, not only why it is socially interesting.

### Theory and Hypotheses

For each hypothesis, separate:

- Institutional or theoretical premise.
- Economic mechanism.
- Competing explanation.
- Observable implication.
- Boundary condition.

Avoid hypotheses that merely restate a coefficient sign without explaining the mechanism.

### Research Design Prose

Use this order:

`sample and setting -> variable construction -> model -> fixed effects and clustering -> identification logic -> interpretation boundary`

State whether the design supports association, causal interpretation under assumptions, mechanism evidence, or prediction.

### Results Narrative

Use this order:

`test purpose -> coefficient or pattern -> economic magnitude if supplied -> interpretation -> limitation`

Do not report statistical significance, coefficients, sample counts, or robustness outcomes unless the user supplies them.

## Claim Calibration

Use stronger verbs only when justified:

- `document`, `find`, or `show` for supplied empirical patterns.
- `is consistent with` for mechanism evidence that is indirect.
- `suggests` for weaker or exploratory evidence.
- `estimates the effect of` only when the design and assumptions plausibly support causal language.

Avoid unsupported novelty language such as "first", "unique", "proves", "fully addresses", or "eliminates endogeneity" unless verified and defensible.

## Output Contract

For writing tasks, return:

- Draft or revised text in the requested language.
- Claim-evidence notes for major claims when useful.
- Assumptions and missing inputs.
- Suggested claim weakening where evidence or identification is insufficient.

For manuscript-facing English prose, default to clear academic English rather than Nature/CNS imitation. For Chinese discussion, keep planning comments in Chinese unless the user asks otherwise.

## QA Checklist

- Every strong claim has supplied evidence, verified literature support, or an explicit assumption.
- Causal language matches the empirical design.
- The accounting or finance relevance is explicit.
- Contribution claims are bounded against prior literature and institutional setting.
- Results are not invented from planned tests.
- The text does not drift into reviewer response, submission strategy, PPT, or reference-management work.
